You noticed a **mistake on your Vietnam eVisa name**—a missing middle name, reversed given and family names, or a typo you only caught days before your flight. This is one of the highest-anxiety visa problems travelers face, and for good reason: airlines deny boarding when eVisa records do not match the passport in their system.

**We are a private visa assistance service—not the Government of Vietnam.** Vietnamese immigration authorities issue and correct eVisas. We help you assess the error, choose the fastest fix, and re-file when a new application is required.

## Quick answer: name mistakes on Vietnam eVisa

| Situation | Risk level | Usual fix |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Typo in surname or given name vs passport | **High** — boarding denial likely | Correction before approval, or new application |
| Middle name missing but on passport MRZ | **High** | Same as above—do not assume it is optional |
| Accent or hyphen difference (José vs Jose) | **Medium–High** | Match passport print exactly in any re-filing |
| Name order reversed (family/given swapped) | **High** | Re-file with names in passport order |
| Approved eVisa matches passport; old booking email wrong | **Low** | Use the correct PDF; update airline profile if needed |

**Golden rule:** The name on your eVisa approval PDF must match the passport you carry at check-in—not an old passport, not a frequent-flyer profile, not how you sign emails.

## Why name typos cause denied boarding

International carriers flying to Vietnam run departure control checks against immigration data. Your eVisa is linked to a **passport number** and **name string**. When those strings disagree—even by one letter—check-in staff often cannot override the mismatch.

Failures happen at three points:

1. **During application** — automated rejection or request to fix data
2. **At airline check-in** — boarding pass withheld
3. **At Vietnam immigration** — entry refused even if the airline let you fly

The worst case is approval with a wrong name: you paid, you received an email, but the document is not valid for travel.

## Common name errors travelers make

### Missing middle name

Many passports—especially from India, the United States, and the UK—print a middle name in the visual zone and MRZ. If your eVisa shows only first and last name, immigration systems may flag a mismatch.

**Check:** Does your MRZ line include more than two name segments? If yes, your eVisa should reflect the same structure.

### Reversed family and given names

Some travelers type given name into the surname field (or vice versa) because everyday usage differs from passport order. Always follow **passport print order**, not how your ticket or hotel booking lists your name.

### Accent marks and special characters

Type names exactly as shown on the passport bio page. If the passport uses plain ASCII (no accents), do not add accents on the form. If accents are printed, include them consistently.

### Maiden name vs married name

Apply using the passport you will **physically present** at the border. If you recently renewed or changed name, use the new booklet—not an old approval tied to a cancelled passport.

## What to do right now (step by step)

### If your application is still processing

1. Contact support **immediately** with your reference number
2. Attach a sharp scan of your passport bio page (MRZ visible)
3. List the exact fields that are wrong and the correct spelling from the passport
4. Do **not** open a second paid application without guidance—duplicates can trigger fraud screening

Corrections before final approval are sometimes possible when caught early.

### If you already received an approval PDF

1. Compare every name field on the PDF to your passport MRZ
2. If any field differs, **do not fly** with that PDF as your only authorization
3. Contact support about correction options or a rush re-application
4. Select [Super Urgent (1 day)](/apply) or **Urgent (3 days)** if your departure date allows

Serious name errors after approval usually require a **new application and new government fee**.

## Name vs passport number errors

A wrong **passport number** is equally serious—often worse because the eVisa is electronically keyed to that number. If both name and number are wrong, treat it as a full re-application case.

If only the name is wrong but the passport number is correct, still contact support before travel—do not self-assess risk at the airport.

## How to prevent name mistakes on your next application

- Read names from the **MRZ** (bottom two lines of the bio page), not from memory
- Do not copy from old airline profiles or OTA bookings
- For children, verify against the child's passport—not the parent's spelling habit
- After approval, open the PDF and cross-check before booking non-refundable hotels
