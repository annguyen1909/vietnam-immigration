Vietnam’s festival calendar blends **lunar New Year (Tet)**, imperial performances in **Hue**, and neighbourhood lantern nights from **Hanoi** to **Hoi An**. Travelling during celebrations rewards flexibility: trains sell out, cities empty then refill with fireworks, and street food prices stay modest in **VND** if you carry cash. This 2026 guide maps the major events, realistic budgeting, and etiquette so you participate without stepping on sacred moments.

## Tet Nguyen Dan (Lunar New Year)

**Tet** reshapes the country for roughly a week of public holidays (exact dates shift on the lunar calendar). Kitchens steam **banh chung** (sticky rice cakes); **peach blossoms** brighten northern homes while **apricot blooms** signal spring hopes in the south.

### Transport and closures

Workers return to family villages before **Tet Eve**, tightening buses, trains, and flights. After the first days, cities exhale—fireworks schedules vary by ward. Expect **museum and government desk closures**; schedule visa validity and support contacts before you fly. Learn concise blessings elders appreciate—**“chuc mung nam moi”** goes far.

### What visitors can enjoy

- Flower markets (**cho hoa**) in **Hanoi**, **Ho Chi Minh City**, and **Can Tho**
- Public fireworks viewing zones (follow police barriers)
- Temple visits with incense offerings—dress covered shoulders/knees

## Hue Festival and imperial heritage

On festival years, **Hue** stages royal-inspired music, **ao dai** parades, and **Perfume River** performances against the **Citadel** silhouette. Between mega-events, nightly dragon boats and vegetarian Buddhist intervals still punctuate the former capital.

### Sites and timing

- **Imperial City (Dai Noi)**—allow a full day; combined tickets bundle zones
- **Royal tombs**—**Khai Dinh**, **Tu Duc**, **Minh Mang** pair well with boat or scooter loops
- **Modest dress** for sacred halls; remove hats indoors

Licensed guides run **300,000–600,000 VND** half-days—worth it for court ritual context. Sunrise tomb visits beat afternoon heat shimmer on the river.

## Mid-Autumn Festival (Tet Trung Thu)

Children parade **lion dances** beneath carp- and star-shaped lanterns. **Hanoi’s Old Quarter**, **Hoi An**, and **Ho Chi Minh City** districts sell mooncakes—sweet lotus paste for gifting, mixed-nut savoury wheels for sharing.

### Family-friendly tips

Plastic toy stalls crowd sidewalks—mind bags in delighted crowds. Some workshops demonstrate bamboo lantern frames if you stay past peak evening hours. Noise curfews tighten residential alleys after **21:00**—keep balcony music low.

## Hung Kings Commemoration (Gio To Hung Vuong)

Spring pilgrimages honour legendary founders at **Phu Tho** shrine complexes—incense smoke, palanquin processions, and quiet zones near altars. Day trips from **Hanoi** need early starts; combine with **My Lam** hot springs if routing north.

## Vesak (Phat Dan)

**Vesak** brightens pagoda courtyards with lotus candles and bodhisattva tableaux. Volunteer queues distribute vegetarian portions—confirm **VND** donations at labelled counters rather than informal touts.

## Respectful participation checklist

- Queue calmly at pagoda gates; never skip security lines
- Ask before filming elders during household rituals
- Applause often suffices for street performers—tip only where jars or programmes allow
- Offer bus seats to parents with toddlers under lantern arches
- Carry **offline maps** when cellular networks choke in fireworks crowds
