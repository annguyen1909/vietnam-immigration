Planning a loop through Southeast Asia—or even a long weekend in Cambodia between Hanoi and Da Nang? A **single-entry Vietnam eVisa** works for straightforward fly-in, fly-out holidays. The moment you **exit Vietnam's border** and plan to **return**, you usually need a **multiple-entry eVisa**. Getting this wrong is one of the costliest visa mistakes: travelers discover at a bus station or airport that their single-entry visa was consumed on the way out, and a new application—full fees, processing time, and stress—is the only fix.

This 2026 guide explains how multiple-entry eVisas work, which itineraries require them, what immigration checks on re-entry, and how the $25 government fee difference compares to reapplying mid-trip.

**We are a private visa assistance service—not the Government of Vietnam.** We help you choose the right visa product at checkout; we do not issue visas or guarantee re-entry. Final decisions belong to immigration officers at the border.

## Single vs multiple entry: plain language

The difference is not subtle—it is binary at the border.

| Type | What you can do | Government fee (90-day product) |
| --- | --- | ---: |
| **Single entry** | Enter Vietnam **once** during the validity window. After you leave the country, the visa is usually **consumed**. | **$55** |
| **Multiple entry** | Leave and **re-enter Vietnam** multiple times while the visa remains valid, if each visit respects printed stay limits. | **$80** |

"Multiple entry" does **not** mean:

- Unlimited total months living in Vietnam
- Automatic work permission
- Entry at any port regardless of your PDF
- A free pass to ignore stay length rules

It means **multiple lawful international border crossings** within the **valid from / valid until** dates on your approval.

### The one-question decision test

Ask yourself: **Will I cross out of Vietnam internationally at any point and come back before my trip ends?**

- **No** → Single entry is enough.
- **Yes** → Multiple entry.
- **Maybe / not sure yet** → Multiple entry costs $25 more in government fees—usually cheaper than a panicked second application abroad.

## Validity window vs duration of stay: two different numbers

Your approval PDF shows concepts travelers routinely confuse. Mixing them up causes overstays and re-entry problems.

| Concept | What it means | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Valid from / valid until** | Calendar window when you **may enter** Vietnam | Valid 1 Apr – 30 Jun 2026 |
| **Duration of stay** | Maximum **days per entry** or total authorized stay | 30 or 90 days per visit (as printed) |

**Rules that follow:**

- You must **enter before** the validity end date
- Each visit must respect the **duration of stay** printed on the PDF
- **Overstaying** a visit harms future entries—even on multiple-entry visas
- Extensions are a separate process—not automatic. See [Vietnam eVisa extension guide](/blog/vietnam-evisa-extension-guide-2026)

### Worked example

You hold a **multiple-entry tourist eVisa**, valid 1 May – 31 July 2026, 30 days per entry:

1. Enter 5 May at **HAN** — you may stay up to 30 days from entry stamp
2. Exit 20 May to Cambodia — first visit ends within limit
3. Re-enter 25 May at **Moc Bai** land border — new entry stamp; new 30-day window begins (subject to officer calculation and printed rules)
4. Must complete final exit before **31 July** validity ends

Always read **your** PDF wording—field labels vary slightly by issuance period.

## Entry ports on every crossing—not just the first

Multiple entry authorizes **re-crossing** the border. It does **not** suspend port rules.

Each arrival must use an **approved port** on your eVisa:

- International airports (e.g., **HAN**, **SGN**, **DAD**)
- Designated land gates (e.g., **Moc Bai**, **Lao Bao** when listed)
- Authorized seaports for cruise arrivals

**Example:** First arrival **Noi Bai (HAN)** by air, second arrival **Moc Bai** by bus from Cambodia. That works **only if** your PDF authorizes both entry types and ports—and you hold **multiple entry**.

Wrong port on any leg = same consequences as a first-arrival mismatch. Full port guide: [Vietnam eVisa entry ports](/blog/vietnam-evisa-approved-entry-ports-2026).

## What immigration checks on re-entry

Second and third arrivals get real scrutiny. Officers verify:

| Check | Re-entry implication |
| --- | --- |
| Passport number matches eVisa | Same booklet as application |
| Visa still within **valid until** date | Expired visa = refusal |
| **Multiple entry** authorization | Single-entry holders cannot return |
| Previous visit did not **overstay** | Overstay stamps hurt re-entry |
| Stay allowance remaining | Per PDF rules and entry stamp |
| Purpose consistency | Tourism vs business answers align with visa |

**Practical habits:**

- Carry **printed approval** on every international leg—not just the first
- Some airlines ask for eVisa on **outbound and return** flights to Vietnam
- Keep photos of entry/exit stamps if you dispute dates later

## Tourism vs business: multiple entry for both

Both tourist and business eVisas offer multiple-entry products at the **same government fee tier** ($80 multiple vs $55 single on our portal).

## Can you upgrade single to multiple after approval?

**Generally no.** Immigration systems tie entry type to the issued PDF. After approval:

- You **cannot** reliably upgrade at the border by paying a fee
- You **cannot** edit entry type through airline staff
- You typically must **apply again** with multiple entry (full fees, new processing time)

**Before first departure:** If you realize your itinerary needs multiple entry, contact support immediately with your reference number. Options may be limited after submission.

**After a rejected upgrade attempt mid-trip:** You may be stranded outside Vietnam until a new eVisa approves—apply from abroad with realistic [processing time](/blog/vietnam-evisa-processing-time-2026) expectations.
