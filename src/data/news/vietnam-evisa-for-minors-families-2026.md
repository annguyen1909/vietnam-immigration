## Does my child need a separate Vietnam eVisa?

If your child has their **own passport**, they typically need their **own eVisa**—including infants and lap children on airline tickets. Immigration authorization is tied to passport numbers, not airline seating.

**US passport book vs passport card:** Vietnam eVisa filing requires a **passport book** (the booklet with a bio page and MRZ). A **passport card** alone is not accepted for international air travel to Vietnam—children need a full passport book before you **apply for a visa**.

**Common false assumptions:**

- "She is only six months old" → still needs her own eVisa if she has her own passport
- "He is a lap infant on our ticket" → lap status is an airline fare category, not an immigration exemption
- "We applied for mum and dad" → children with passports are not covered by parent approvals
- "Our nationality is visa-exempt" → exemption rules may still require an eVisa for longer stays, multiple entry, or specific passport types

**Rare exceptions** involve specific bilateral **visa exemptions** for certain nationalities on short stays—not a general rule you should rely on at the airport. Confirm the child's passport country on [Vietnam visa eligibility by country](/check-requirement) before you fly.

Children listed on a **parent's passport** (where that practice still exists for some countries) follow different rules—apply using the exact travel document the child will present at immigration. When in doubt, match the document, not the family relationship.

### Visa-exempt children: when families still need an eVisa

Some families skip applications because **one parent** qualifies for visa-free entry. Children do not inherit exemption by association—each passport is judged on its own nationality and rules.

| Situation | Typical planning outcome |
| --- | --- |
| **Whole family on exempt passports, short stay** | eVisa may not be required—confirm exemption days and entry type |
| **Mixed nationalities in one family** | Exempt parent may enter freely; non-exempt child still needs authorization—common when one parent holds a **Hong Kong** or UK passport and another does not |
| **Stay longer than exemption allows** | Each traveler who will exceed exemption needs an eVisa |
| **Side trip to Cambodia/Thailand then return** | Multiple-entry eVisa usually required even if first entry was exempt |
| **UK, EU, or other partial exemptions** | Exemption length and conditions change—verify live before departure |

**Official policy signal:** Exemption rules are set by Vietnamese immigration authorities. **Planning advice:** When any family member needs a visa, file the **whole group's entry data consistently**—same entry port, aligned dates—even if one passport is technically exempt.

## Documents to prepare for every child

Gather a folder per child before you open the form. Stopping mid-application to chase a baby photo is how date-of-birth typos happen.

| Document | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| **Passport bio scan** | Full colour page, flat, all corners visible, no fingers on edges |
| **Passport photos** | Plain background, eyes open; avoid pacifiers blocking the face when possible |
| **Guardian / parent names** | Match passports of traveling adults |
| **Travel dates** | Align with adults—mixed arrival dates confuse airlines |
| **Passport validity** | Six months beyond arrival; blank page for stamp |

### Infant and toddler photo tips

Baby **passport photos** are harder than adult shots—but the technical rules match [Vietnam eVisa photo requirements](/blog/vietnam-evisa-photo-requirements-2026):

- Lay the infant on a **white sheet** in even natural light
- Shoot from above with face clearly visible
- Avoid **parent shadows** or hands in frame
- Retake if the face is blurry—automated checks reject soft images
- Some studios offer infant passport sessions if home shots fail repeatedly

### Newborn and recently issued passports

If the passport was issued days before travel:

- Triple-check the **MRZ prints correctly**—new booklets sometimes have manufacturing quirks
- Confirm the passport number in the system matches the physical booklet
- Allow **extra processing margin**—fixes are harder with imminent flights

### Passports expiring within six months

Renew before applying—allow time to **apply for a passport** for any child whose booklet expires within six months of arrival. A family cannot board if one child's passport fails airline validity checks—even when parents hold approved eVisas. Build renewal and **processing times** into your [Vietnam eVisa processing time](/blog/vietnam-evisa-processing-time-2026) calendar.

## Guardianship, consent, and airline requirements

Vietnam eVisa application forms focus on **applicant identity data**—they are not a full custody review. Immigration at the border may still ask questions when adults are not parents or when surnames differ.

**Documents to carry (recommended):**

| Situation | Suggested carry-on document |
| --- | --- |
| **Surname differs from parent** | Birth certificate copy (English or certified translation) |
| **One parent traveling alone** | Notarized **consent letter** from non-traveling parent—airlines often require this regardless of visa |
| **Legal guardianship** | Court order or guardianship papers |
| **Adopted children** | Passport name must match application exactly; adoption papers if surname recently changed |
| **School group** | School authorization letter; each student still needs individual eVisa |
| **Grandparent or relative escort** | Parental consent letter naming the traveling adult |

**Planning advice:** Airline policies on minor travel can be stricter than immigration. Check your carrier's unaccompanied-minor and single-parent rules separately from eVisa filing. Low-cost carriers in Southeast Asia sometimes enforce consent letters on international legs even when Vietnam immigration does not ask.

## Matching entry ports, dates, and visa type across the family

All family members should share:

- The **same entry port** on each approved PDF (matching your shared flight or border crossing)
- The **same visa category** (tourism vs business) unless a specific passport nationality limits options
- The **same entry type** (single vs multiple) when traveling together on one itinerary

**Failure pattern:** Parents receive PDFs listing **Da Nang** while a child's PDF lists **Hanoi** because applications were filed on different days with inconsistent data. Families flying into **Ho Chi Minh City** (Tan Son Nhat) or heading north to **Ha Long Bay** after landing must still match the port printed on every PDF—not the city you visit first. Immigration may process the family at different speeds—or flag the mismatch.

**Single vs multiple entry for families:**

| Itinerary | Recommended entry type |
| --- | --- |
| One resort stay, no regional exits | Single entry ($55 govt fee) |
| Thailand/Cambodia side trip then return to Vietnam | **Multiple entries** ($80 govt fee) |
| Cruise disembark/re-embark | Multiple entry—confirm with cruise operator |
| Long stay with possible extension | Max 90-day product at filing; extensions are not guaranteed—see [Vietnam eVisa extension guide](/blog/vietnam-evisa-extension-guide-2026) |

## Processing time for family groups

**Processing times** for family groups follow the same authority review queue as solo travelers—passengers are reviewed **sequentially or in parallel** depending on volume. Family **visa applications** do not always approve in one batch email.

**What slows family groups:**

- One child's **photo fails** validation—the correction email may reference the whole group reference
- **Inconsistent dates** trigger manual review
- **Peak holiday volume** (December–January, **Tet**, summer) extends timelines for everyone
- **New passports** with MRZ formatting quirks on one child's booklet

**Planning timeline for families:**

| Days before departure | Recommendation |

## At Vietnam immigration and airport check-in with children

Airlines and immigration both verify **each child individually**. Parents with valid PDFs have been stopped because a child's authorization was missing or listed the wrong port.

**Check-in (airline counter):**

- Present **printed eVisa + passport** for every traveler, including infants
- Airlines may ask for **consent letters** before they check immigration documents
- Lap infants with their own passport still need their own PDF—do not fold them into a parent's file

**Immigration (on arrival):**

- One officer may process the family together, but each passport is stamped individually
- Keep children **with parents** in the queue—split queues slow families when data mismatches surface
- Have **birth certificates** ready if surnames differ; officers ask more often than the eVisa form suggests

**Land borders and family vans:**

- Every child needs the same **authorized port** as adults—family vans crossing from Cambodia or Laos have been turned back when one PDF lists a different gate
- Paper copies matter when mobile signal is weak at remote crossings

For port-specific rules, see [Vietnam eVisa entry ports](/blog/vietnam-evisa-approved-entry-ports-2026).

## Special cases families should plan for

### Dual citizenship children

Apply with the passport the child will **present entering Vietnam**. Do not mix data from two nationalities across one application. If one passport qualifies for exemption and the other does not, choose the document you will actually carry at immigration.

### Divorced parents and different surnames

Passport name governs the form. Carry birth certificates and consent letters even when the eVisa form does not ask for them—airlines and immigration may.

### School and sports groups

Each student needs an **individual eVisa**. Group organizers should collect passport scans early and standardize entry port and dates across the roster. Airlines may want school letters in addition to visas.

### Adopted children and recent name changes

The application must match the **current passport bio page** exactly. If adoption paperwork updated the name after passport issuance, use the new passport before applying.

### Teenagers traveling with one parent

Same consent rules as younger children for many airlines—especially for travelers under **age 16** on some carriers. The teenager still needs their own eVisa on their own passport.

### Multi-generational trips (grandparents + parents + kids)

Up to 15 passengers can share one group checkout, but **each generation** needs individual uploads. Grandparents filing for grandchildren should carry consent naming them as traveling guardians.

### Long family stays beyond 90 days

Standard eVisas authorize up to **90 days**. In-country extensions are limited and not guaranteed—each family member would need to extend individually if policy allows. Read [Vietnam eVisa extension guide](/blog/vietnam-evisa-extension-guide-2026) and plan duration at initial filing instead of assuming extra months on arrival.

## After approval: preparing the family for departure

When emails arrive—check **spam folders** for every approval:

1. **Download each traveler's PDF** separately; label files by name
2. **Print one copy per person**—airline staff sometimes ask individually
3. **Save PDFs offline** on phones—not only in email
4. **Verify every PDF**: name, passport number, entry port, entry type
5. **Pack consent and birth certificates** if your family situation warrants them
6. **Screenshot reference codes** in case email filters block messages on travel day
7. **Cross-check against flight tickets**—especially when entry port is Da Nang, Phu Quoc, or a land border

At check-in, airlines may verify **each child** even when parents hold valid PDFs. At immigration, present the passport used in that child's application with their printed eVisa.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does a baby need a Vietnam eVisa?

With their own passport, usually yes—even as a lap child. Confirm nationality rules on [eligibility by country](/check-requirement).

### Can the whole family apply together?

Yes—up to 15 passengers in one session on our flow, with individual uploads per traveler.

### Are child government fees cheaper than adult fees?

No. Government fees follow visa type ($55 single / $80 multiple), not age. See [/fees](/fees) for your group total.

### What should parents carry at the border?

Printed eVisas and passports for everyone. Birth certificate copies and consent letters when surnames or custody arrangements warrant them.

### Can children share one eVisa with a parent?

No. Each passport requires its own authorization.

### Should children have the same entry port as parents?

Yes. Every PDF should list the port matching your shared arrival flight or border.

### How early should families apply?

At least **7–14 days** before departure; more if infant photos may need retakes.

### What if only one child's application is rejected?

Fix that child's document or data before the group travels. Do not assume the family can board on parent approvals alone.

### Do visa-exempt children need an eVisa?

Only if their stay exceeds exemption limits, they need multiple entry, or their passport nationality requires a visa. Verify each child separately.

### Can grandparents file a Vietnam eVisa for a grandchild?

A guardian can complete the form using the child's passport data. Carry parental consent naming the traveling adult at check-in.
