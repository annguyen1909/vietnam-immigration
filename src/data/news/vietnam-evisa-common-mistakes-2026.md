This guide explains the ten errors travelers make most often in 2026, why each one matters under official immigration rules, and exactly how to fix them before you pay—or before you board your flight.

## Why small mistakes become big problems

A Vietnam eVisa links electronically to your passport number. Airlines scan passenger data before boarding international flights to Vietnam. Immigration officers compare your physical passport, your printed or digital eVisa, and their system records at the border.

When any field disagrees—name spelling, passport number, date of birth, entry port, or entry type—the chain breaks at one of three points:

1. **Automated validation** during application (rejection email)
2. **Airline check-in** (boarding denied)
3. **Immigration control** (entry refused even if the airline let you fly)

The frustrating part: many mistakes are avoidable with five minutes of careful review against your passport bio page. The expensive part: fixing errors after approval often means a **new application and new fees**.

## Mistake 1: Passport number typos

Transposing digits, dropping leading zeros, or confusing the letter **O** with the number **0** is the single most common Vietnam eVisa mistake.

Your passport number appears twice on the bio page: in the visual zone and in the **machine-readable zone (MRZ)**—the two lines of chevron characters at the bottom. The MRZ is what automated systems read first.

**How to avoid it:**

- Open your passport to the bio page and read the MRZ **character by character**
- Type the number into the form once, then read it aloud against the passport
- Never copy from an old booking, a frequent-flyer profile, or a photo of your passport from years ago
- Keep leading zeros exactly as printed—some countries use numbers that start with zero

**Real scenario:** A traveler types `AB1234567` when the MRZ shows `AB01234567`. The application may be rejected, or worse, approved with the wrong number—then the airline denies boarding because the eVisa does not match the passport in their system.

## Mistake 2: Wrong date of birth format

Date-of-birth errors are almost always a **format habit problem**, not a memory problem. US and Canadian travelers often default to month/day/year. Many passports—and the Vietnam eVisa form—use **day/month/year (DD/MM/YYYY)**.

**How to avoid it:**

- Copy the date exactly as printed on your passport bio page
- Do not convert from how you write birthdays in everyday life
- For children, double-check the parent's instinct against the child's passport print—not a birth certificate date format

Cross-check against the MRZ, where the date appears in a compressed YYMMDD format. If your typed DOB and the MRZ disagree, stop and fix before payment.

## Mistake 3: Entry port on approval does not match your ticket

This mistake surprises travelers who assume any Vietnamese airport works once they have an eVisa. **Official rule:** you must enter at the **port of entry printed on your approved eVisa PDF**.

On many assisted application flows—including ours at [/apply](/apply)—you do not select an entry port during the form. The authorized port appears on your **approval PDF after processing**. That means your job is not finished when you pay; you must **verify the port against your flight ticket** before you fly.

**Common failure pattern:**

- Approval lists **Noi Bai (Hanoi)** but your ticket lands at **Tan Son Nhat (Ho Chi Minh City)**
- Approval lists a **land border** but you booked a flight to **Da Nang**
- Family members have **different ports** on their PDFs because applications were filed separately with inconsistent data

**How to avoid it:**

- Know your first international landing in Vietnam before you apply
- After approval, open every traveler's PDF and confirm the port matches the ticket
- If the port is wrong, contact support immediately—do not assume immigration will make an exception

## Mistake 4: Single-entry visa used for a leave-and-return itinerary

Southeast Asia trips often include a side trip to Thailand, Cambodia, or Laos—and then a return to Vietnam. A **single-entry** eVisa authorizes **one arrival period**. Once you exit Vietnam, that entry is consumed.

**Government fees on our portal (2026):**

| Entry type | Government fee | Best for |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| **Single entry (90 days)** | **$55** | One continuous stay in Vietnam |
| **Multiple entry (90 days)** | **$80** | Leave and re-enter within the validity window |

The extra **$25** government fee for multiple entry is cheaper than a second visa application, missed flights, or rebooking hotels because you cannot re-enter.

**How to avoid it:**

- Map your route on paper: every exit and re-entry to Vietnam
- Cruises that disembark in Vietnam and re-board later often need multiple entry—confirm with your operator
- Read your PDF **entry type** field before booking regional flights

## Mistake 5: Photo that is not passport-style

Casual selfies, sunglasses, patterned walls, heavy beauty filters, and cropped faces fail both automated checks and human review. Vietnam eVisa photo rules align with standard passport-style portraits.

**Typical photo failures:**

- Face too small in the frame
- Shadow across eyes or MRZ-like glare on glasses
- Hat or non-religious headwear
- Old photo that no longer resembles you at immigration

**How to avoid it:**

- Use a plain light background, face forward, eyes open
- Take a new photo if your hair or appearance changed significantly
- For infants, lay the baby on a white sheet in even light—avoid parent shadows in frame

Full specifications: [Vietnam eVisa photo requirements](/blog/vietnam-evisa-photo-requirements-2026).

## Mistake 6: Expired or short-validity passport

Most airlines and immigration officers expect passports valid for at least **six months beyond your arrival date** in Vietnam, with at least one blank page for entry stamps—a common planning standard even when your eVisa is approved. Applying with a passport that expires soon—or renewing after approval—creates a mismatch.

**How to avoid it:**

- Check expiry date before you start the form
- Renew early if you are inside the six-month window
- Apply with the **exact passport** you will carry at immigration—not an old cancelled booklet

If you renew after receiving an eVisa, the approval tied to the old passport number is generally **no longer valid**. Plan passport renewals before visa filing.

## Mistake 7: Tourism visa for unauthorized work

A tourist eVisa is for leisure travel—holidays, sightseeing, family visits, and short visits that fit tourism purpose. It does **not** authorize:

- Local employment or teaching in Vietnam
- Paid work for Vietnamese clients while physically in the country
- Activities that immigration would classify as business if you selected tourism to save complexity

**How to avoid it:**

- Select **business** on the form if your trip is meetings, conferences, or commercial visits without local employment
- Remote work for employers outside Vietnam is a gray area—honest purpose selection matters
- Repeated business travel on tourist visas raises scrutiny

Compare channels if your trip is not standard tourism: [Vietnam eVisa vs embassy visa](/blog/vietnam-evisa-vs-embassy-visa-2026).

## Mistake 8: Ignoring email after submission

Approval letters, correction requests, and payment confirmations go to the email address you typed on the form. A typo in your email—or an inbox that filters government senders to spam—can make you think your visa is "stuck" when it was approved days ago.

**How to avoid it:**

- Use an email you check daily, including on mobile
- Search spam and promotions folders for **72 hours** after applying
- Add the sender to contacts if your provider aggressively filters unknown addresses
- For families, use one monitored lead email for group applications

If processing exceeds your selected tier window, read [Emergency Guide: eVisa Stuck Processing](/troubleshooting/stuck-processing) before submitting a duplicate application.

## Mistake 9: No printed copy at check-in

Travel forums debate whether digital copies suffice. In practice, **airlines and immigration officers often ask for paper**. Phone batteries die. PDF viewers fail offline. Check-in agents work quickly and prefer a physical document.

**How to avoid it:**

- Print **one copy per traveler** after approval
- Save the PDF offline on your phone (not only in email)
- Screenshot the approval reference code as backup

This costs little and prevents unnecessary delays at the gate.

## Mistake 10: Applying too late

Standard handling on assisted flows is commonly **within five business days** after payment. Vietnamese public holidays—especially **Tet**—can slow review even when portals stay online. Document correction loops add days.

**Recommended planning window:**

| Days before departure | Risk level | Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **14+** | Low | Ideal—apply with final passport and itinerary |
| **7–13** | Moderate | Reasonable for standard processing |
| **3–6** | High | Use urgent tiers only if eligible; fix uploads immediately if rejected |
| **0–2** | Very high | Emergency—contact support; have backup plans |

Applying the same day you fly is how travelers reach check-in without a PDF. Read [Vietnam eVisa processing time](/blog/vietnam-evisa-processing-time-2026) before you choose a processing tier.

## Bonus mistake: Duplicate applications

Paying twice because the first application "seemed slow" creates conflicting records in immigration systems. Duplicate submissions with the same passport can trigger **fraud or security screening**, delaying both applications or causing refusals.

**How to avoid it:**

- Save your **reference number** immediately after payment
- Contact support with payment proof before opening a parallel application
- Track one application through to approval or formal rejection

If the first application was rejected, fix the root cause before reapplying—see [Vietnam eVisa rejection reasons](/blog/vietnam-evisa-rejection-reasons-2026).

## What happens at the airport if you made a mistake?

Airlines verify travel authorization before boarding. Immigration police have the final say even if the airline let you fly.

| Mistake | Typical airport outcome |
| --- | --- |
| MRZ / passport number mismatch | Boarding denied at check-in |
| Wrong entry port on PDF | Supervisor delay or entry refusal |
| Expired passport | No boarding; visa fees generally not refunded by airline |
| Single entry after regional exit | Re-entry denied on return leg |
| Name spelling mismatch | Check-in hold or immigration questioning |

**Planning advice:** Arrive at the airport early if you know there is a minor discrepancy. Do not expect immigration to waive official rules because your holiday is booked.

## Business travelers: extra pitfalls

Tourism eVisas are for leisure. Business travelers often trip over:

- Listing a **conference sponsor** as employer without selecting business purpose
- Carrying **client contracts or local invoices** that suggest work immigration did not authorize
- Importing **equipment** that needs separate permits

Repeated business trips may eventually need embassy stickers or longer-stay categories—see [/embassy](/embassy) for embassy processing options.

## Families: mistakes multiplied

Family trips amplify individual errors:

- Applying only for parents while children have their own passports
- **Mismatched entry dates** between children and adults
- Child portrait with a **parent's shadow** or pacifier obscuring the face
- Different **entry ports** across family PDFs

Each child needs their own authorization. Group filing tips: [Vietnam eVisa for families and minors](/blog/vietnam-evisa-for-minors-families-2026) and [family group Vietnam eVisa FAQ](/faq/family-group-vietnam-evisa).

## Pre-flight verification checklist (5 minutes)

Run this list the night before your flight:

1. Passport validity **6+ months** beyond arrival date
2. eVisa PDF **name** = passport name (including middle names)
3. eVisa **passport number** = MRZ on physical passport
4. **Entry port** = ticket destination airport or land border
5. **Single vs multiple entry** matches itinerary
6. **Printed copy** + offline PDF on phone for every traveler
7. **Email approval** saved in case airline asks for reference

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the most common Vietnam eVisa mistake?

Passport number typos and date-of-birth format errors against the MRZ. Read the machine-readable lines at the bottom of your bio page character by character before you pay.

### Can I enter Vietnam at any airport with an eVisa?

No. You must arrive at the port listed on your approval PDF. See [Vietnam eVisa entry ports](/faq/vietnam-evisa-entry-points) for planning.

### Can I reuse a single-entry Vietnam eVisa?

Usually not for a second entry after you have left Vietnam. Choose multiple entry if your itinerary includes a exit and return.

### Do I need a printed copy of my Vietnam eVisa?

Yes—paper plus an offline PDF on your phone is the safest combination for airlines and immigration.

### What if I notice a mistake after approval?

Contact support immediately. Minor issues may have solutions before travel; serious typos in name or passport number often require a new filing.

### How early should I apply to avoid timing mistakes?

Apply **7–14+ days** before departure when possible. Do not book non-refundable hotels until you hold the approved PDF.

### Do children need separate eVisas?

Yes—each passport holder needs their own authorization. Parents cannot cover infants or children on one adult eVisa.

### Will duplicate applications speed up approval?

No. Duplicate paid applications can slow or block approval. Track one reference number and contact support first.
